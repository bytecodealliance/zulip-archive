Chris Fallin (Nov 23 2025 at 19:47):

Hi @Vinh -- thanks for commenting!

We do indeed aspire to mentor folks, and we want to be as welcoming as possible. That's where our documentation comes from. That said, we're also currently in a situation where the core contributors on Wasmtime and Cranelift are stretched very thin with existing work and probably don't have the time for "classical mentoring" (walking you through how to build a thing, teaching how everything works, teaching how the language itself works, etc) -- or at least that's the case for me, if your level is still "learning Rust".

If others (Alex, Nick, Pat, ...?) want to chime in and volunteer as an active mentor, of course by all means don't let me stop you! But I just wanted to set likely expectations.

I'd recommend working through the interpreters book and any other project ideas you have with good Rust intro material (there's a lot out there!) and getting comfortable with both the language and the concepts in interpeters, compilers, and runtimes. If you're built a few toy instances of those and feel comfortable with them, you're a lot more likely to be able to pick up one of our tasks and be productive in our codebase.

All the best!

Chris Fallin (Nov 24 2025 at 05:06):

Gotcha -- thanks for asking here and props for wanting to explore this area!

Aside from Crafting Interpreters that you mentioned, a few general directions to explore:

  • Definitely get comfortable in idiomatic Rust, ideally by writing interpreters/compilers on your own when following along with books on those topics
  • Low-level/"systems" Rust is useful generally for work in Wasmtime (Cranelift is more "classical algorithmic Rust" but does have some perf tricks too). The Rustnomicon, or Phil Opp's OS dev series, or something in that vein would be good to understand unsafe Rust, how low-level data structure implementations work, and the like
  • Classical compilers algorithms are still really important in Cranelift work -- it's a massive tome but the SSA and dataflow analysis parts of the Dragon Book (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compilers:_Principles,_Techniques,_and_Tools) are good background. You'll want to understand SSA, domtrees and graph traversal orders and graph theory in general, how analyses and passes work in general
  • I haven't read it but "Wasm from the ground up" is apparently a good book introducing WebAssembly as a bytecode platform

Once someone has background in all the above then Cranelift and Wasmtime are definitely more approachable. All the best!

Alexa VanHattum (Dec 09 2025 at 22:30):

A few other slightly belated recommendations (coming from a swe-turned-academic/Cranelift contributor):

  • Some folks at Brown have an experimental version of the Rust book that includes interactive quizzes. This can be nice to check your understanding as you go: https://rust-book.cs.brown.edu
  • Adrian Sampson's "self-guided" advanced compilers course is entirely online, and has some great videos with modern summaries of compilers topics (SSA, domtrees, dataflow, etc). The exercises are in principal language agnostic, with a JSON-based IR, so you could complete them in Rust: https://www.cs.cornell.edu/courses/cs6120/2025fa/self-guided/
